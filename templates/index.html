About Cervical Screening

What is cervical screening?

Cervical screening (previously known as a "smear test") is a free health test available on the NHS that helps prevent cervical cancer. It checks for a virus called high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV) and, if found, examines the cells for any changes.

It's not a test for cancer, but rather a preventative measure that helps identify any potential issues before they develop into cancer.

Who should get screened?

  • All women and people with a cervix aged 25 to 64
  • Ages 25-49: Every 3 years
  • Ages 50-64: Every 5 years
  • You'll receive an invitation letter when it's time for your screening

What happens during the screening?

1

Before your appointment

You'll be asked to undress from the waist down and lie on a bed with a sheet over you.

2

During the test

A nurse will gently insert a speculum and use a small soft brush to take a sample from your cervix.

3

After the test

Your sample is sent to a lab and you'll receive results by letter, usually within 2 weeks.

The test should take less than 5 minutes. The nurse is there to help you feel comfortable and answer any questions.

You can request a female nurse if that makes you more comfortable. Many clinics also offer evening and weekend appointments.

Common Myths & Facts

"I don't need screening if I've had the HPV vaccine"

MYTH: The HPV vaccine provides complete protection against cervical cancer.

FACT: While the HPV vaccine provides excellent protection against the most common cancer-causing HPV types, it doesn't protect against all types. Regular screening is still essential even if you've been vaccinated.

"Cervical screening is painful"

MYTH: Cervical screening is always painful.

FACT: Most people find screening uncomfortable but not painful. The procedure takes just a few minutes. You can ask for a smaller speculum, and talk to your nurse about ways to make it more comfortable.

"I don't need screening if I'm not sexually active"

MYTH: Screening is only needed if you're currently sexually active.

FACT: If you've ever been sexually active, you should attend screening. HPV can remain dormant for many years before causing cell changes. Even if you're no longer sexually active or have had the same partner for years, screening is still important.

"A positive HPV test means I have cancer"

MYTH: Testing positive for HPV means you have cancer or will definitely develop it.

FACT: HPV is very common, and most infections clear on their own. A positive HPV test means you have the virus that can sometimes cause cell changes. These changes take years to develop into cancer, which is why regular screening is so effective at prevention.

"I'm embarrassed about my body"

MYTH: Nurses judge your body during cervical screening.

FACT: Healthcare professionals perform many screenings every day and are focused only on your health. They've seen all body types and are trained to make you feel comfortable. You can request a female nurse, bring someone with you for support, or wear a skirt that can stay on during the procedure.

Common Questions

Find answers to frequently asked questions about cervical cancer screening.

What is cervical cancer screening?

Cervical cancer screening is a way to detect abnormal cells on the cervix that could later develop into cervical cancer. In the UK, screening includes:

  • Primary HPV testing to check for high-risk types of human papillomavirus (HPV)
  • Cytology (looking at the cells) if HPV is found

This is sometimes referred to as "cervical smear" or "smear test", though the testing methods have evolved over time.

Why do I need to get screened for cervical cancer?

Cervical screening is important because it can:

  • Detect HPV infections and cell changes before they become cancerous
  • Prevent cancer from developing
  • Find cancer at an early stage when it's easier to treat
  • Save lives - regular screening prevents up to 70% of cervical cancer deaths
At what age should I start cervical cancer screening?

In the UK, the NHS Cervical Screening Programme invites:

  • Women and people with a cervix aged 25 to 64 for regular screening
  • First invitations are sent at age 25
  • Those aged 65+ are only invited if they haven't been screened since age 50 or have had recent abnormal results

The age range is based on evidence about when screening is most effective. People under 25 are not routinely screened because cervical cancer is very rare in this age group, and cell changes are common and usually resolve on their own.

How often do I need to get screened for cervical cancer?

The NHS recommends different screening frequencies based on age:

  • Ages 25-49: Every 3 years
  • Ages 50-64: Every 5 years

If you have had previous abnormal results or are at higher risk, you may be invited more frequently. Always follow the guidance in your screening invitation letter.

What is the difference between a Pap smear and an HPV test?

The two tests examine cells collected from the cervix in different ways:

  • Pap smear (cytology): Examines cervical cells under a microscope to look for abnormal changes that might be precancerous or cancerous.
  • HPV test: Looks for the presence of high-risk types of HPV that can cause cervical cancer.

In the UK, the NHS now uses HPV testing as the primary screening method. If HPV is found, the same sample is then checked for abnormal cells (cytology).

What happens during a cervical cancer screening test?

During a cervical screening appointment:

  • A trained healthcare professional (usually a practice nurse) will explain the procedure
  • You'll undress from the waist down and lie on an examination couch with your knees bent and apart (a paper sheet can cover your lower body)
  • The nurse will gently insert a speculum (a smooth, tube-shaped tool) into your vagina to hold it open
  • A small, soft brush is used to take a sample of cells from your cervix
  • The sample is sent to a laboratory for testing

The test usually takes less than 5 minutes, though the entire appointment may last around 10-15 minutes.

What are the risks or discomforts of cervical cancer screening?

Cervical screening is generally safe, but may involve:

  • Discomfort: Some people experience mild discomfort, cramping, or pressure during the procedure
  • Light bleeding: Spotting or light bleeding for a day or two after screening is normal
  • Anxiety: Some people find the procedure embarrassing or stressful

You can ask the healthcare provider to:

  • Use a smaller speculum
  • Take more time
  • Allow you to insert the speculum yourself
  • Provide additional support if you have experienced trauma or have specific concerns
Are there any signs or symptoms of cervical cancer that I should watch for?

Common symptoms of cervical cancer may include:

  • Unusual vaginal bleeding (between periods, after sex, or after menopause)
  • Changes to vaginal discharge (heavier, different color/smell)
  • Pain or discomfort during sex
  • Unexplained lower back or pelvic pain
  • Unexplained fatigue

Important: These symptoms can be caused by many conditions other than cancer. However, if you experience any of these symptoms, contact your GP for advice - don't wait for your next screening appointment.
